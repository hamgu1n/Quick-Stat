# Unit 1 – Data Collection and Experimental Design

## Section 1.1 – Populations and Samples

In statistics we are often trying to answer questions about a population. You can think of a population as the target demographic we are interested in. Examples of population include: college students, employees at a company, or children ages 8–12. One of the most crucial aspects of statistics stems from our inability to examine a whole population of thousands of employees, millions of students, or over a billion children. Instead we sample populations, or take a small portion of the population to examine and draw conclusions about.

## Section 1.2 – Biased Sampling

Samples can be categorized as **biased** and **unbiased**. A biased sample is a sample where one portion of a population is favored or weighted more than others. Two common examples of biased samples include the **convenience sample** and the **voluntary response sample**. A convenience sample describes a sample of the easiest individuals to obtain, such as your friends, your coworkers, or the first 10 people to walk through a door. As you may notice these samples are by no means random and often contain large quantities of similar people.

A voluntary response sample only includes individuals who choose to include themselves in the study. This can include product surveys or reviews, RateMyProfessor comments, or surveys by companies to receive in store credit. Like a convenience sample, this can often lead to large numbers of the same types of people, for instance more outgoing people, individuals in need of class credits, or people motivated by quick cash.

## Section 1.3 – Unbiased Sampling

In statistics, a good sample is measured by how representative it is of the general population we are examining. These are called **unbiased samples** and include: **stratified samples**, **systematic samples**, **cluster samples**, and **simple random samples**.

The most common sampling technique is the **simple random sample (SRS)**. An SRS consists of randomly selecting members of a population for your sample, as if picking names out of a hat.

Unlike a simple random sample, that does not require any framework, a **systematic sample** requires a predetermined method for sampling. Examples of a systematic sample include sampling every third person down a list of names, every fifth customer to enter a store, or every tenth house on a street. To reduce bias, the starting point of a sample is randomized.

Another example of an unbiased sample is a **stratified sample**. A stratified sample aims to reflect the demographic structure of a population. For instance, a researcher may want to explore the sleeping habits of students at Roanoke College. Rather than simply selecting 50 students at random, the researcher may emphasize the importance of grade level in the sample. Using previously known demographic breakdowns, the researcher can randomly select a proportional number of students from each grade level.

Similar to stratified sampling, **cluster sampling** divides populations into groups called clusters. However, unlike stratified samples, cluster samples randomly select groups rather than individual observations. For instance, we can divide Roanoke College into clusters using dorm buildings. We can then select our sample by selecting whole dorm rooms, or rather all the students who live in a dorm building. We often use cluster sampling when our population is extremely large or spread out.

Each of these sampling methods has its place depending on the research question, the available resources, and the structure of the population. As we will later see, the choice of sampling method can have real consequences for the conclusions we draw from data.

### Key Points – Populations and Samples
- A population is the full group we want to study; a sample is the portion we actually observe
- Biased samples over-represent some groups — convenience samples and voluntary response samples are common examples
- Unbiased sampling methods include simple random, systematic, stratified, and cluster sampling
- The goal of any sampling method is to produce a sample that accurately represents the population
- Choice of sampling method depends on the structure of the population and available resources
