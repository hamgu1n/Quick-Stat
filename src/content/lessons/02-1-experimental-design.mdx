## Section 2.1 – Observational vs Experimental Studies

In statistics, we collect data through two methods of study. You are most likely familiar with experimental studies like your science experiments, where we explore the effects of a treatment (amount of baking soda) on some kind of response (the strength of a volcanic eruption). Experimental studies are extremely valuable as they allow us to determine cause-and-effect relationships between two variables of interest by controlling conditions.

Unlike experimental studies that can show direct cause-and-effect relationships, **observational studies** focus on associations. An observational study is when researchers observe individuals and measure variables without any influence or direct change in a situation. They simply record findings as they happen. While less powerful in that they cannot establish causation, observational studies thrive when experimental studies are unethical or too large a scale to conduct.

For example, we may want to study the relationship between sleep and academic performance. It would be unethical to tell students to deprive themselves of sleep for days on end. Instead we can survey students on their sleep each day and compare it to their grades over time, without ever directly changing a student's habits.

## Section 2.2 – Randomization, Control, and Blinding

When designing experiments, it's crucial we adhere to three major principles: **randomization**, **control**, and **blinding**.

**Randomization** is the process of randomly assigning participants or subjects into treatment groups. Randomization of treatment groups ensures that we avoid hidden biases from affecting our results. Additionally, it allows us to balance unknown variables and prevent systematic bias.

Suppose we were interested in testing the effects of a new pain medication. Our sample consists of 50 individuals, 25 males and 25 females. Suppose we chose to split our treatments so that the 25 males received the pain medication and the 25 females received the placebo. For starters, we would lose any information on the effects of the medication on women. Moreover, we would have no real information on the effects it has on men as we have no benchmark to relate to. Instead we should have randomly assigned our treatment groups.

**Control** refers to keeping other variables constant throughout an experiment. This way we can trust that any observed difference is truly from the change in treatment. This is often why experiments contain a **control group**, which does not receive the treatment.

**Blinding** refers to removing a participant's knowledge of their own treatment group. For example, in our earlier example on pain medication: after randomly assigning our treatment groups, if we were to tell our participants if they were given the medication or a placebo, we would surely see a difference in their reactions and survey answers.

The above example is an instance of a **single-blind study**, in which participants are not told of the treatment they are receiving. A **double-blind study** describes when neither the participant nor the researcher is aware of a participant's treatment group. Blinding helps prevent the participant and/or researchers' expectations from influencing the results.

Together, randomization, control, and blinding help to strengthen the validity of an experiment by reducing **confounding** (outside and unwanted) factors.

### Key Points – Experimental Design
- Experimental studies assign treatments and can establish causation; observational studies only show association
- Randomization prevents hidden biases from contaminating treatment groups
- Control keeps all other variables constant so differences can be attributed to the treatment
- Blinding prevents expectations from skewing results — single-blind hides treatment from participants, double-blind hides it from both participants and researchers
- A control group provides the baseline needed to measure a treatment's effect
- Confounding factors are outside variables that can distort results if not accounted for
