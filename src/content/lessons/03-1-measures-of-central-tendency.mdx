Descriptive statistics allow us to "describe" or summarize data in a meaningful way. Often we summarize data by describing the *center* and *spread* of a dataset which we will explore throughout this chapter.

## Section 3.1 – Measures of Central Tendency

The most common summary of a dataset is describing its *center*. The most useful measures of central tendency are the *mean*, *median*, and *mode*.
Each provides different information, and while some are more commonly used than others, no single measure is universally best.

### The Mean

The mean, often called the average, is the sum of all observations, divided by the number of observations. We often notate the mean with $\bar{x}$, pronounced "x-bar". You may see the formula for the mean written as:

$$
\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i
$$

### The Median

The median is the middle value of an ordered list. For example the list $[1, 2, 4, 5, 6]$ has a median of $4$. In the case of an even number of entries the median is the average of the two center values.

Below is a list of 5 students study hours:

{/* ```r
set.seed(1)
sort(as.integer(rnorm(5, mean = 3, sd = 1)))
``` */}

The median of the dataset is 3. After sampling another student we now have:

{/* ```r
set.seed(1)
sort(as.integer(rnorm(6, mean = 3, sd = 1)))
``` */}

Since the list now has an even number of data points the median of the dataset is now the average of the two center values, 2.5.

{/* ```r
set.seed(1)
data <- data.frame(
  scores = rnorm(50, mean = 75, sd = 10)
)

ggplot(data, aes(x = "", y = scores)) +
  geom_boxplot(fill = "lightgray", width = 0.3) +
  stat_summary(
    fun = median,
    geom = "point",
    size = 4
  ) +
  labs(
    title = "Exam Scores",
    subtitle = "Median highlighted in the boxplot",
    y = "Score",
    x = ""
  ) +
  theme_textbook()
``` */}

Unlike the mean, who is vulnerable to outliers, the median is considered *robust* against outliers. For example, is a dataset of student attendance over a 2 weeks in a computer science course.

{/* ```r
set.seed(2)
sort(c(c(0, 0),as.integer(rnorm(8, mean = 25, sd = 1))))
``` */}

Comparing the mean and median of the above dataset, we find that the mean is 19.6, while the median is 24. We can see that the mean was "pulled down" by the the two days where 0 students attended the class due to a blizzard. As we will later see, outliers are not necessarily insignificant but can greatly shift the descriptive statistics of a dataset.

The median does have its downsides however, it requires that your list be sorted which can be tedious by hand and computationally tasking for larger datasets.

### The Mode

The mode is the most frequent value in a list. A dataset may have more than one mode if multiple values have the same frequency, or no mode if all values appear equally. The mode is especially useful for **qualitative data**.

### Key Points – Measures of Central Tendency
- Mean: includes all data but sensitive to outliers
- Median: middle value, robust against outliers
- Mode: most frequent value, useful for qualitative data
- Choice depends on data type and presence of outliers
