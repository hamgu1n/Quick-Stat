## Section 3.3 – Measures of Position and Outliers

Knowing the center and spread of a dataset is helpful, but sometimes we want to know where a specific value falls *relative to the rest of the data*. Measures of position answer questions like: "Was a score of 78 good or bad on this exam?" or "Is this employee's salary unusually high?" The three most commonly used measures of position are **percentiles**, **quartiles**, and **z-scores**.

### Percentiles and Quartiles

A **percentile** tells you the percentage of observations that fall at or below a given value. For example, if your exam score is at the 80th percentile, it means you scored higher than roughly 80% of students who took the exam. Percentiles are especially common in standardized testing and pediatric growth charts.

**Quartiles** divide a sorted dataset into four equal parts. The **first quartile** ($Q_1$) is the 25th percentile — 25% of the data falls below it. The **second quartile** ($Q_2$) is the median, or the 50th percentile. The **third quartile** ($Q_3$) is the 75th percentile — 75% of the data falls below it. Together, $Q_1$, $Q_2$, and $Q_3$ give a compact summary of where the bulk of the data lives, and they form the foundation of the five-number summary we will explore in the next section.

### Z-Scores

A **z-score** (also called a standard score) measures how many standard deviations a particular value is from the mean. The formula is:

$$
z = \frac{x - \bar{x}}{s}
$$

where $x$ is the individual value, $\bar{x}$ is the mean, and $s$ is the standard deviation.

A z-score of 0 means the value is exactly at the mean. A z-score of 2 means the value is two standard deviations *above* the mean, and a z-score of -1.5 means the value is 1.5 standard deviations *below* the mean. Z-scores allow us to compare values from different distributions — for example, how does a score of 88 in one class compare to a score of 74 in another, if the two classes had different means and spreads?

### Outlier Detection Using the IQR Rule

An **outlier** is a value that is unusually far from the rest of the data. Outliers can arise from measurement errors, data entry mistakes, or genuinely unusual observations — and as we saw with the CEO example in the previous section, they can pull the mean dramatically. One formal way to identify outliers is the **IQR rule**.

A value is considered a potential outlier if it falls below the **lower fence** or above the **upper fence**:

$$
\text{Lower Fence} = Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}
$$

$$
\text{Upper Fence} = Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}
$$

Any observation outside these fences is flagged as a potential outlier. This rule does not mean the value should be removed — context always matters — but it gives us a consistent, objective starting point for investigation.

{/* ```r
data_outlier <- c(12, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20, 21, 55)
Q1 <- quantile(data_outlier, 0.25)
Q3 <- quantile(data_outlier, 0.75)
IQR_val <- Q3 - Q1
lower <- Q1 - 1.5 * IQR_val
upper <- Q3 + 1.5 * IQR_val
cat("Lower fence:", lower, "  Upper fence:", upper)
``` */}

### Key Points – Measures of Position
- Percentiles describe where a value ranks relative to the rest of the distribution
- Quartiles ($Q_1$, $Q_2$, $Q_3$) divide sorted data into four equal parts
- Z-scores express how many standard deviations a value sits above or below the mean
- Z-scores allow comparison across distributions with different means and spreads
- The IQR rule flags potential outliers using fences set at $Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ and $Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$
- Flagged outliers should be investigated, not automatically discarded
