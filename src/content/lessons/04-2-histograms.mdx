## Section 4.2 – Histograms

When we move from categorical data to **numerical** (quantitative) data, bar charts are no longer the right tool. We need a way to visualize the *distribution* of a continuous variable, how observations are spread across a range of values. The **histogram** is the most fundamental tool for this job.

### What Is a Histogram?

A histogram divides the range of a numerical variable into equal-width intervals called **bins** (or classes), then draws a bar for each bin whose height represents how many observations fall within that range. Unlike bar charts, the bars in a histogram touch each other, this reflects the fact that the underlying data is continuous, with no gaps between possible values.

The choice of bin width matters enormously. Too few bins and you lose all the detail, the data looks like a featureless lump. Too many bins and the histogram becomes jagged and noisy, making patterns hard to see. In practice, statistical software chooses a reasonable default, but it is always worth experimenting. ### Describing the Shape of a Distribution

One of the main purposes of a histogram is to reveal the **shape** of a distribution. We describe shape using four key characteristics:

**Symmetric**: The left and right halves of the histogram are approximate mirror images. A perfect bell-shaped curve is symmetric. In symmetric distributions, the mean and median are close together.

**Skewed right** (positive skew): The histogram has a long tail stretching to the right. A few unusually large values pull the mean to the right of the median. Income distributions are a classic example, most people earn moderate incomes, but a few earn very high incomes.

**Skewed left** (negative skew): The histogram has a long tail stretching to the left. Exam scores in an easy class often look like this, most students score high, but a few score very low.

**Unimodal vs. bimodal**: A histogram with one clear peak is **unimodal**. A histogram with two peaks is **bimodal**, which often suggests the data comes from two distinct subgroups mixed together.

### Key Points – Histograms
- Histograms visualize the distribution of **numerical** data by grouping values into bins
- Bars touch because the data is continuous; height represents frequency or relative frequency within each bin
- Bin width affects how much detail is visible, too few or too many bins obscures the true shape
- Distributions are described as symmetric, skewed right (long right tail), or skewed left (long left tail)
- In a right-skewed distribution the mean is greater than the median; in a left-skewed distribution the mean is less than the median
- Bimodal histograms (two peaks) may indicate that two distinct groups are present in the data
