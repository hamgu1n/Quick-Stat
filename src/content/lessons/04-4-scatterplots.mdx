## Section 4.4 – Scatterplots

Every graph we have looked at so far has displayed a single variable at a time — its distribution, its center, its spread. But many of the most interesting questions in statistics involve the *relationship* between two variables. Does studying more lead to higher grades? Do taller people tend to weigh more? Does higher caffeine consumption correlate with better test performance? To explore these questions visually, we use a **scatterplot**.

### What Is a Scatterplot?

A **scatterplot** displays pairs of numerical observations as points on a coordinate plane. One variable is placed on the horizontal axis (the **explanatory variable**, also called the independent variable) and the other on the vertical axis (the **response variable**, also called the dependent variable). Each dot on the plot represents one observation in the dataset.

When reading a scatterplot, we focus on three features of the relationship:

1. **Direction**: Does the relationship go upward (as $x$ increases, $y$ tends to increase — a **positive association**) or downward (as $x$ increases, $y$ tends to decrease — a **negative association**)?
2. **Form**: Is the pattern roughly **linear** (the points follow a straight-line trend) or **curved**?
3. **Strength**: How closely do the points follow the trend? Are they tightly packed around an imaginary line, or are they loosely scattered? We will quantify strength more precisely in Chapter 10 using correlation coefficients.

### Outliers in Scatterplots

Just as we look for outliers in single-variable distributions, we also watch for them in scatterplots. An outlier in a scatterplot is a point that falls far from the overall trend — either because its $x$ value, its $y$ value, or both are unusual. Some outliers are also called **influential points** because, as we will see in Chapter 11, they can dramatically shift the line we fit through the data.

It's important not to remove outliers automatically. Sometimes they represent data entry errors; other times they are genuine and scientifically important observations that deserve special attention.

### Key Points – Scatterplots
- Scatterplots display the relationship between two numerical variables; each point represents one observation
- The explanatory variable goes on the x-axis; the response variable goes on the y-axis
- Describe a relationship by its direction (positive or negative), form (linear or curved), and strength (tight or loose)
- A positive association means both variables tend to move in the same direction; a negative association means they move in opposite directions
- Outliers in a scatterplot fall far from the overall trend and may be influential points
- Association does not imply causation — a lurking variable may drive both observed variables
