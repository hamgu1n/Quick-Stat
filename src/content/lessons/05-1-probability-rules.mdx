# Unit 3 – Probability and the Central Limit Theorem

## Section 5.1 – Probability Rules

Everything we have done so far — describing samples, summarizing distributions — has been purely descriptive. We described what *did* happen. Probability takes us in a different direction: it describes what *could* happen and how likely each outcome is. Probability is the language of uncertainty, and it forms the foundation for everything in statistical inference that follows.

### Basic Probability

The **probability** of an event is a number between 0 and 1 that measures how likely the event is to occur. A probability of 0 means the event is impossible; a probability of 1 means it is certain. In a **random experiment** — a process whose outcome is uncertain — the set of all possible outcomes is called the **sample space**, often denoted $S$.

For equally likely outcomes, the probability of an event $A$ is:

$$
P(A) = \frac{\text{number of outcomes in } A}{\text{total number of outcomes in } S}
$$

For example, rolling a fair six-sided die has a sample space of $S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$. The probability of rolling an even number is $\frac{3}{6} = 0.5$, because three outcomes (2, 4, 6) satisfy the event.

### Complement, Union, and Intersection

Three operations on events appear constantly in probability:

The **complement** of event $A$, written $A^c$ (or $\bar{A}$), is the set of all outcomes *not* in $A$. The complement rule states:

$$
P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)
$$

The **union** of events $A$ and $B$, written $A \cup B$, includes every outcome that is in $A$, in $B$, or in both. The **addition rule** gives us:

$$
P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)
$$

We subtract $P(A \cap B)$ because otherwise we count the overlap twice.

The **intersection** of $A$ and $B$, written $A \cap B$, includes only the outcomes that are in *both* $A$ and $B$ simultaneously.

Two events are **mutually exclusive** (also called **disjoint**) if they cannot both occur at the same time — their intersection is empty. For mutually exclusive events, the addition rule simplifies to:

$$
P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)
$$

### Independent Events

Two events $A$ and $B$ are **independent** if the occurrence of one does not change the probability of the other. For independent events, the **multiplication rule** states:

$$
P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)
$$

Independence is a strong assumption. Flipping a coin twice is a classic example — the result of the first flip has no bearing on the second. Whether a randomly selected student passed two different unrelated courses might also be approximately independent. But whether a student passed a prerequisite and then passed the follow-on course almost certainly are *not* independent.

### Key Points – Probability Rules
- Probability is a number between 0 and 1; the sample space contains all possible outcomes
- $P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$ — complement rule
- $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$ — addition rule; simplifies when $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive
- Mutually exclusive events cannot both occur; their intersection is empty
- Independent events satisfy $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$
- Independence and mutual exclusivity are not the same concept — mutually exclusive events with positive probability are actually *dependent*
