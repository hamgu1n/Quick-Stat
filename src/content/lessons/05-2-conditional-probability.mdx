## Section 5.2 – Conditional Probability

In the previous section, we calculated probabilities under the assumption that we knew nothing about the outcome ahead of time. But in real life, we often have *partial information* that changes our assessment of how likely something is. If you know that it rained last night, the probability of seeing wet grass this morning is much higher than it would be otherwise. This idea — updating a probability given new information — is called **conditional probability**.

### Definition

The **conditional probability** of event $A$ given that event $B$ has occurred is written $P(A \mid B)$, read "the probability of $A$ given $B$." The formula is:

$$
P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}, \quad \text{provided } P(B) > 0
$$

Intuitively, we are restricting our attention to the world where $B$ has already happened (the denominator), and asking what fraction of that world also contains $A$ (the numerator).

In a class of 40 students, 15 are seniors, 10 are seniors who play a sport, and 20 students overall play a sport.

Given that a student plays a sport, what is the probability that they are a senior?

**Example 5.2.2 – Conditional Probability from a Table**

A survey of 200 college students asked whether they own a laptop and whether they have a part-time job:

|  | Has a job | No job | Total |

| Owns laptop | 80 | 60 | 140 |

### Conditional Probability and Independence

In the previous section we said events $A$ and $B$ are independent if $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$. We can also express independence in terms of conditional probability: $A$ and $B$ are independent if and only if

$$
P(A \mid B) = P(A)
$$

In other words, knowing $B$ occurred tells us nothing about the probability of $A$. This is the intuitive definition of independence — new information doesn't change the odds. If $P(A \mid B) \neq P(A)$, the events are dependent.

<div className="example">
**Example 5.2.3 – Testing Independence via Conditional Probability**

In a group of 100 people, 50 exercise regularly and 40 report good sleep quality. 30 people both exercise regularly and report good sleep quality.

Is exercising regularly independent of reporting good sleep quality? Use conditional probabilities to support your answer.
</div>

### Bayes' Theorem

Sometimes we know $P(A \mid B)$ but we really want $P(B \mid A)$ — the probability in the reverse direction. This is where **Bayes' Theorem** comes in. It is one of the most important results in all of probability and statistics:

$$
P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B)}{P(A)}
$$

A common application is in medical testing. Suppose a disease affects 1% of the population, and a test correctly identifies the disease 95% of the time. If you test positive, what is the probability you actually have the disease? The answer — often surprisingly low — illustrates why understanding conditional probability matters deeply in real-world decision making.

<div className="example">
**Example 5.2.4 – Bayes' Theorem: Medical Testing**

A rare illness affects 2% of a population. A test for the illness has a 90% true positive rate (probability of a positive test given the disease is present) and a 5% false positive rate (probability of a positive test given the disease is absent).

If a randomly selected person tests positive, what is the probability they actually have the illness?

(Hint: Let $D$ = has disease, $T^+$ = tests positive. You are looking for $P(D \mid T^+)$.)
</div>

A bag contains 3 red and 2 blue marbles. Two marbles are drawn one at a time *without replacement*.

1. What is the probability that the first marble is red?
2. Given that the first marble was red, what is the probability that the second marble is also red?

### Key Points – Conditional Probability
- Conditional probability $P(A \mid B)$ gives the probability of $A$ assuming $B$ has occurred
- Formula: $P(A \mid B) = P(A \cap B) / P(B)$
- Events are independent if $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$ — knowing $B$ gives no information about $A$
- Bayes' Theorem lets us reverse the direction of conditioning: $P(B \mid A) = P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B) / P(A)$
- Two-way tables are a useful tool for computing conditional probabilities from survey data
- Without replacement sampling creates dependent events — the probability changes after each draw
