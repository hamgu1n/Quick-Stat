## Section 6.1 – Sampling Distributions

Suppose you want to know the average GPA of all students at your university. You can't survey every single student, so you take a random sample of 50 students and calculate their average GPA. That average is a **statistic** — a number calculated from your sample. Now imagine taking a different sample of 50 students tomorrow. You'd get a slightly different average. Do it again, and again, and the averages will keep varying. The **sampling distribution** describes the pattern of that variation across all possible samples.

### Parameters vs. Statistics

Before going further, it's important to distinguish two concepts that are easy to confuse:

A **parameter** is a number that describes the entire *population*. It is fixed but usually unknown. For example, the true average GPA of all students at a university is a parameter — often denoted $\mu$ (the population mean).

A **statistic** is a number calculated from a *sample*. It changes from sample to sample. The sample mean $\bar{x}$ is a statistic. We use statistics to *estimate* parameters — and understanding how statistics vary is what sampling distributions are all about.

### The Sampling Distribution of the Mean

When we take many random samples of size $n$ from a population and calculate the mean of each sample, those means form their own distribution — the **sampling distribution of the sample mean** $\bar{x}$.

Two key facts govern this distribution:

1. The **mean of the sampling distribution** equals the population mean: $\mu_{\bar{x}} = \mu$. This tells us that $\bar{x}$ is an **unbiased estimator** of $\mu$ — on average, it hits the right target.

2. The **standard deviation of the sampling distribution** (called the **standard error**) is:

$$
\sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}
$$

where $\sigma$ is the population standard deviation and $n$ is the sample size.

Notice that as $n$ increases, the standard error *decreases* — larger samples produce sample means that cluster more tightly around the true population mean. This is one of the most important ideas in all of statistics: bigger samples give us more reliable estimates.

### Key Points – Sampling Distributions
- A parameter describes a population (fixed, usually unknown); a statistic describes a sample (varies from sample to sample)
- The sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ describes how sample means vary across all possible samples of size $n$
- The mean of the sampling distribution equals the population mean: $\mu_{\bar{x}} = \mu$
- The standard error is $\sigma_{\bar{x}} = \sigma / \sqrt{n}$ — it decreases as sample size increases
- Larger samples produce less variable (more reliable) estimates of the population mean
- Understanding sampling variability is the foundation of all statistical inference
