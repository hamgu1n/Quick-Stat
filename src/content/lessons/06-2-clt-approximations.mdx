## Section 6.2 – Approximations Using the Central Limit Theorem

In the previous section we established that the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ has mean $\mu$ and standard error $\sigma / \sqrt{n}$. But what *shape* does that distribution have? The answer — one of the most remarkable results in all of statistics — is the **Central Limit Theorem (CLT)**.

### The Central Limit Theorem

**The Central Limit Theorem** states: regardless of the shape of the population distribution, the sampling distribution of the sample mean $\bar{x}$ will be approximately **normal** for sufficiently large sample sizes.

More precisely: if $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ are independent observations drawn from *any* population with mean $\mu$ and finite standard deviation $\sigma$, then for large $n$:

$$
\bar{x} \sim N\!\left(\mu,\; \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)
$$

The word "approximately" is key. A common rule of thumb is that $n \geq 30$ is usually sufficient for the CLT to apply, though the required sample size depends on how non-normal the original population is. Highly skewed or heavy-tailed populations may need larger samples.

This result is remarkable because it allows us to use the normal distribution as a tool for inference about *any* population — not just normally distributed ones. It is the reason why so many statistical procedures rely on the normal distribution.

### Using the Normal Approximation

Once we know the sampling distribution is approximately normal, we can use what we know about the standard normal distribution to compute probabilities. We convert $\bar{x}$ to a z-score using the standard error:

$$
z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}
$$

From there, we can use a z-table or software to find probabilities. This is the same z-score formula we saw in Section 3.3, but applied to a sample mean rather than an individual value.

### Key Points – CLT Approximations
- The Central Limit Theorem states that the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ is approximately normal for large $n$, regardless of the population's shape
- The approximation improves as $n$ increases; $n \geq 30$ is a common guideline, but skewed populations may need more
- The CLT allows us to use normal probability calculations for any population as long as the sample is large enough
- The standardized sample mean is $z = (\bar{x} - \mu) / (\sigma / \sqrt{n})$
- The CLT is the foundation for confidence intervals and hypothesis tests, which we explore in Units 4 and beyond
