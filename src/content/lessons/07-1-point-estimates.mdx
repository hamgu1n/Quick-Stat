# Unit 4 – Statistical Inference

## Section 7.1 – Point Estimates

We have spent the last several chapters learning how to describe data and understand the behavior of sample statistics. Now we turn to the central challenge of statistics: using a sample to draw conclusions about a population. This is called **statistical inference**, and it comes in two main flavors: estimation and hypothesis testing. We begin with estimation.

### What Is a Point Estimate?

A **point estimate** is a single value, calculated from a sample, that we use as our best guess for an unknown population parameter. It is called a "point" estimate because it gives one specific number rather than a range.

The most common point estimates are:

| Population Parameter | Symbol | Sample Statistic (Point Estimate) | Symbol |
|---|---|---|---|
| Population mean | $\mu$ | Sample mean | $\bar{x}$ |
| Population proportion | $p$ | Sample proportion | $\hat{p}$ |
| Population standard deviation | $\sigma$ | Sample standard deviation | $s$ |

For example, if we want to estimate the average commute time for all workers in a city, we survey 100 workers and calculate their average commute time. That sample mean is our point estimate of $\mu$.

### Properties of Good Estimators

Not every statistic makes a good point estimate. Two properties we want in an estimator are:

**Unbiasedness**: An estimator is **unbiased** if, on average across all possible samples, it equals the true parameter. The sample mean $\bar{x}$ is an unbiased estimator of $\mu$ — as we saw in Section 6.1, the mean of the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ equals $\mu$. This is why we divide by $n - 1$ rather than $n$ when computing the sample variance $s^2$ — it makes $s^2$ an unbiased estimator of $\sigma^2$.

**Efficiency**: Among all unbiased estimators, we prefer the one with the smallest variance (the one whose values cluster most tightly around the parameter). A more efficient estimator is less affected by sampling variation.

An important limitation of any point estimate is that it comes with no indication of how accurate it is. A single number gives no sense of the uncertainty involved. This is why we almost always accompany a point estimate with a **margin of error** or a **confidence interval** — the subject of the next section.

### Key Points – Point Estimates
- A point estimate is a single sample statistic used to estimate an unknown population parameter
- Common point estimates: $\bar{x}$ estimates $\mu$; $\hat{p}$ estimates $p$; $s$ estimates $\sigma$
- A good estimator is unbiased (correct on average) and efficient (low variance)
- Point estimates vary from sample to sample — they are subject to sampling variability
- A point estimate alone gives no indication of precision; a confidence interval is needed to convey uncertainty
