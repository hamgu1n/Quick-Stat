## Section 7.2 – Confidence Intervals for Means and Proportions

A point estimate tells us our best single guess for a population parameter. But a single number can't convey how certain or uncertain we are. A **confidence interval** solves this problem by providing a *range* of plausible values for the parameter, accompanied by a specified level of confidence.

### What Is a Confidence Interval?

A **confidence interval (CI)** is an interval of the form:

$$
\text{Point Estimate} \pm \text{Margin of Error}
$$

The **margin of error** quantifies how far the estimate might reasonably be from the true parameter. The width of the interval reflects our uncertainty, a narrower interval means a more precise estimate.

A **95% confidence interval** means that if we were to repeat the sampling process many times and construct a confidence interval each time, approximately 95% of those intervals would contain the true population parameter. It does *not* mean there is a 95% probability that this particular interval contains the parameter, the parameter is fixed; the interval is what varies. This distinction is subtle but important.

### Confidence Interval for a Population Mean (Known $\sigma$)

When the population standard deviation $\sigma$ is known and the sample is large (or the population is normal), we use the **z-interval** for the mean:

$$
\bar{x} \pm z^* \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}
$$

Here $z^*$ is the **critical value** from the standard normal distribution corresponding to the desired confidence level. Common critical values:

| Confidence Level | $z^*$ |
|---|---|
| 90% | 1.645 |
| 95% | 1.960 |
| 99% | 2.576 |

### Confidence Interval for a Population Mean (Unknown $\sigma$)

In practice, $\sigma$ is almost never known, we estimate it using $s$. When the population standard deviation is unknown, we replace $z^*$ with $t^*$, a critical value from the **t-distribution** with $n - 1$ degrees of freedom:

$$
\bar{x} \pm t^* \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}
$$

The t-distribution is bell-shaped and symmetric like the normal, but has heavier tails, reflecting the extra uncertainty introduced by estimating $\sigma$ with $s$. As $n$ increases, the t-distribution approaches the normal distribution. We will explore t-based inference in more detail in Section 8.2.

### Confidence Interval for a Proportion

To estimate a population proportion $p$, we use the sample proportion $\hat{p} = x/n$ (where $x$ is the count with the characteristic of interest). The confidence interval is:

$$
\hat{p} \pm z^* \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}
$$

The term $\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})/n}$ is the standard error of $\hat{p}$. This formula relies on the CLT and requires that both $n\hat{p} \geq 10$ and $n(1-\hat{p}) \geq 10$.

### Key Points – Confidence Intervals
- A confidence interval provides a range of plausible values for a population parameter
- Form: Point Estimate $\pm$ Margin of Error
- The confidence level (e.g., 95%) describes the long-run proportion of intervals that capture the true parameter, not the probability for any single interval
- Use the z-interval when $\sigma$ is known; use the t-interval when $\sigma$ is unknown (replacing $z^*$ with $t^*$)
- The confidence interval for a proportion uses $\hat{p} \pm z^* \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})/n}$; requires $n\hat{p} \geq 10$ and $n(1-\hat{p}) \geq 10$
- Wider intervals reflect more uncertainty; narrower intervals require larger samples
