## Section 8.1 – z-Tests

Confidence intervals estimate the value of a population parameter. **Hypothesis testing** takes a different approach: we start with a specific claim about a parameter and then use sample data to decide whether the evidence supports or contradicts that claim. In this section we introduce the framework for hypothesis testing and apply it using **z-tests**, tests that rely on the standard normal distribution.

### The Hypothesis Testing Framework

Every hypothesis test begins with two competing hypotheses:

The **null hypothesis** ($H_0$) is the default claim, the one we assume to be true unless there is strong evidence against it. It typically states that there is "no effect," "no difference," or that a parameter equals a specific value.

The **alternative hypothesis** ($H_a$ or $H_1$) is the claim we are trying to find evidence _for_. It represents the researcher's suspicion or the claim they want to test.

For example, if a bottling company claims their bottles contain exactly 16 ounces of water, the hypotheses might be:

- $H_0$: $\mu = 16$ (the machine fills bottles correctly on average)
- $H_a$: $\mu \neq 16$ (the machine is not filling correctly)

After formulating the hypotheses, we collect data and compute a **test statistic**, a number that measures how far the observed data are from what $H_0$ predicts. We then compare this test statistic to a **critical value** or compute a **p-value** to decide whether to reject $H_0$.

### The z-Test for a Mean

When the population standard deviation $\sigma$ is known (or $n$ is large enough to use $s$ as a close approximation), the test statistic for a claim about a population mean is:

$$
z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}
$$

where $\mu_0$ is the value claimed in $H_0$. This z-statistic tells us how many standard errors the sample mean falls from the hypothesized mean.

### The p-Value

The **p-value** is the probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one computed, assuming $H_0$ is true. A small p-value means the observed data would be very unlikely if $H_0$ were true, providing evidence against it.

We compare the p-value to a pre-chosen **significance level** $\alpha$ (commonly 0.05):

- If $p$-value $\leq \alpha$: **Reject $H_0$**, the data provide sufficient evidence against the null
- If $p$-value $> \alpha$: **Fail to reject $H_0$**, the data do not provide sufficient evidence

Failing to reject $H_0$ does not mean $H_0$ is true, it simply means we don't have enough evidence to reject it.

### Type I and Type II Errors

No hypothesis test is infallible. We can make two types of errors:

A **Type I error** occurs when we reject $H_0$ when it is actually true, a false positive. The probability of a Type I error equals the significance level $\alpha$. Lowering $\alpha$ makes it harder to reject $H_0$, reducing Type I error risk.

A **Type II error** occurs when we fail to reject $H_0$ when it is actually false, a false negative. Its probability is denoted $\beta$. The **power** of a test is $1 - \beta$: the probability of correctly rejecting a false null hypothesis.

There is always a trade-off: decreasing $\alpha$ (reducing Type I error) increases $\beta$ (increasing Type II error). Larger sample sizes are the most effective way to reduce both.

### Key Points – z-Tests
- Hypothesis testing begins with $H_0$ (null, the default) and $H_a$ (alternative, the research claim)
- The z-test statistic for a mean: $z = (\bar{x} - \mu_0) / (\sigma / \sqrt{n})$
- The p-value measures how surprising the data are under $H_0$; small p-values suggest evidence against $H_0$
- Reject $H_0$ when p-value $\leq \alpha$; fail to reject when p-value $> \alpha$
- Type I error: reject a true $H_0$ (probability = $\alpha$); Type II error: fail to reject a false $H_0$ (probability = $\beta$)
- Failing to reject $H_0$ is not the same as proving $H_0$ is true
