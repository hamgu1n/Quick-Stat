## Section 8.2 – t-Tests

In the previous section we used the z-test when the population standard deviation $\sigma$ was known. In practice, $\sigma$ is almost never known — we estimate it using the sample standard deviation $s$. Substituting $s$ for $\sigma$ introduces additional uncertainty, especially for small samples. The **t-test** accounts for this extra uncertainty by using the **t-distribution** instead of the standard normal.

### The t-Distribution

The **t-distribution** is bell-shaped and symmetric, like the standard normal, but with heavier tails. This reflects the added variability that comes from estimating $\sigma$ with $s$. The shape of the t-distribution depends on the **degrees of freedom** ($df$), which for a one-sample t-test equals $n - 1$.

As $df$ increases (i.e., as $n$ grows), the t-distribution becomes increasingly close to the standard normal. By about $df = 30$ or more, the two are nearly identical — which is why the z-test is often a reasonable approximation for large samples even when $\sigma$ is unknown.

### One-Sample t-Test

The test statistic for a one-sample t-test is:

$$
t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}}
$$

This statistic follows a t-distribution with $n - 1$ degrees of freedom under $H_0$. The steps are the same as for the z-test: state hypotheses, calculate the test statistic, find the p-value using the t-distribution, and draw a conclusion.

### Two-Sample t-Test

Often we want to compare the means of two independent groups — for example, do male and female students spend different amounts of time studying? The **two-sample t-test** (also called an independent samples t-test) tests:

$$
H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_a: \mu_1 \neq \mu_2 \; (\text{or} < \text{or} >)
$$

The test statistic is:

$$
t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}
$$

The degrees of freedom for this test are approximated using the Welch formula (usually computed by software) and need not be memorized.

### Paired t-Test

A **paired t-test** is used when the two samples are not independent — each observation in one group is naturally linked to an observation in the other. Common examples include:
- Before-and-after measurements on the same individuals
- Scores of twins (one in each group)
- Measurements on the same subject under two different conditions

For a paired test, we compute the difference $d_i = x_{1i} - x_{2i}$ for each pair, then apply a one-sample t-test to the differences with $H_0: \mu_d = 0$.

### Key Points – t-Tests
- Use the t-test when $\sigma$ is unknown; the test statistic is $t = (\bar{x} - \mu_0) / (s / \sqrt{n})$ with $df = n - 1$
- The t-distribution has heavier tails than the normal; it approaches the normal as $df$ increases
- The two-sample t-test compares means of two independent groups
- The paired t-test is used when observations are naturally matched; convert to differences and apply a one-sample t-test
- All t-tests follow the same framework: state hypotheses, compute test statistic, find p-value, draw conclusion
- As in the z-test, failing to reject $H_0$ does not prove it is true
