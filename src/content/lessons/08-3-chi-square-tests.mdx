## Section 8.3 – Chi-Square Tests

The z-tests and t-tests we have studied so far are designed for numerical data, where we are making claims about means. But what about categorical data? How do we test whether two categorical variables are related, or whether a categorical distribution matches a particular pattern? For these questions we turn to the **chi-square test** ($\chi^2$ test), pronounced "ky-square."

### The Chi-Square Distribution

The **chi-square distribution** is a family of right-skewed distributions, each determined by its **degrees of freedom**. Unlike the normal or t-distribution, the chi-square distribution takes only non-negative values. As degrees of freedom increase, the distribution shifts right and becomes more symmetric.

The key idea behind all chi-square tests is comparing **observed counts** (what we actually see in the data) to **expected counts** (what we would expect if the null hypothesis were true).

### Chi-Square Test for Goodness of Fit

The **goodness-of-fit test** asks: does a sample come from a population with a specific categorical distribution? For example, does the distribution of M&M colors in a bag match the percentages advertised by the manufacturer?

The test statistic is:

$$
\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}
$$

where $O$ is the observed count in each category and $E$ is the expected count. The expected count for each category is:

$$
E = n \times p_0
$$

where $p_0$ is the hypothesized proportion for that category. The statistic follows a chi-square distribution with $df = k - 1$, where $k$ is the number of categories.

### Chi-Square Test for Independence

The **test for independence** asks: are two categorical variables related, or are they independent of each other? For example, is there a relationship between gender and preferred type of exercise?

We organize the data in a **contingency table** (also called a two-way table) with rows representing categories of one variable and columns representing categories of the other. The expected count for each cell is:

$$
E_{ij} = \frac{(\text{row total}_i) \times (\text{column total}_j)}{\text{grand total}}
$$

The test statistic is the same formula as for goodness of fit, summed over all cells. The degrees of freedom are:

$$
df = (\text{number of rows} - 1) \times (\text{number of columns} - 1)
$$

### Key Points – Chi-Square Tests
- Chi-square tests are used for categorical data, comparing observed counts to expected counts
- The test statistic is $\chi^2 = \sum (O - E)^2 / E$; larger values indicate greater departure from $H_0$
- Goodness-of-fit test: tests whether a single categorical variable matches a specified distribution; $df = k - 1$
- Test for independence: tests whether two categorical variables are related using a contingency table; $df = (r-1)(c-1)$
- Expected counts should be at least 5 in each cell for the test to be valid
- The chi-square distribution is right-skewed and non-negative; p-values are always computed from the right tail
