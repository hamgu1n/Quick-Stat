## Section 9.1 – One-Way ANOVA

So far we have compared the means of two groups using the two-sample t-test. But what if we want to compare three or more groups simultaneously? Suppose a researcher wants to compare the average exam score of students taught using three different methods. We could run multiple t-tests — method A vs. B, A vs. C, B vs. C — but this inflates the probability of making a Type I error. If each test has $\alpha = 0.05$, running three tests raises the overall chance of at least one false positive well above 5%. **Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)** solves this problem by testing all groups at once.

### The ANOVA Idea

**One-way ANOVA** tests whether the means of three or more groups (levels of a single categorical variable, called a **factor**) are all equal. The hypotheses are:

$$
H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \cdots = \mu_k
$$
$$
H_a: \text{at least one mean is different}
$$

The key insight behind ANOVA is that it partitions the total variability in the data into two sources:

1. **Variability between groups** (also called treatment variability): how much the group means differ from the overall mean
2. **Variability within groups** (also called error or residual variability): how much individual observations differ from their own group mean

If the between-group variability is large *relative to* the within-group variability, we have evidence that the group means differ. If most of the variation exists within groups rather than between them, the groups look similar.

### The F-Statistic

The ratio of between-group variability to within-group variability is the **F-statistic**:

$$
F = \frac{\text{Mean Square Between (MSB)}}{\text{Mean Square Within (MSW)}}
$$

where:

$$
\text{MSB} = \frac{\text{Sum of Squares Between (SSB)}}{k - 1}, \quad \text{MSW} = \frac{\text{Sum of Squares Within (SSW)}}{N - k}
$$

Here $k$ is the number of groups and $N$ is the total number of observations. Under $H_0$ (all group means equal), the F-statistic follows an **F-distribution** with degrees of freedom $df_1 = k - 1$ and $df_2 = N - k$.

A large F-statistic means the between-group variation dominates — providing evidence that at least one group mean differs. We reject $H_0$ when the p-value is below $\alpha$.

### Assumptions of One-Way ANOVA

ANOVA rests on three key assumptions:

1. **Independence**: The observations within and between groups are independent. This is generally satisfied by random sampling and random assignment.
2. **Normality**: The data within each group come from a normally distributed population. ANOVA is fairly robust to mild departures from normality, especially with large samples.
3. **Equal variances** (homoscedasticity): The population variances of all groups are equal. A common rule of thumb is that ANOVA is reasonable if the largest sample standard deviation is no more than twice the smallest.

### Key Points – One-Way ANOVA
- ANOVA tests whether the means of three or more groups are all equal; $H_a$ is that at least one mean differs
- Total variability is partitioned into between-group (SSB) and within-group (SSW) components
- The F-statistic = MSB / MSW; a large F suggests the group means differ
- Degrees of freedom: $df_1 = k - 1$ (between), $df_2 = N - k$ (within)
- ANOVA assumes independence, normality within groups, and equal variances across groups
- Rejecting $H_0$ only tells us that *some* means differ — post-hoc tests are needed to identify *which* ones
