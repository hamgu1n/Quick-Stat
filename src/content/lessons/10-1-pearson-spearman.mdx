# Unit 5 – Correlation and Regression

## Section 10.1 – Pearson and Spearman Correlation

In Section 4.4 we used scatterplots to *visualize* the relationship between two numerical variables. Now we want to *quantify* that relationship — to give it a single number that captures both its direction and its strength. That number is called a **correlation coefficient**.

### Pearson's Correlation Coefficient

The most widely used measure of linear association is **Pearson's correlation coefficient**, denoted $r$. It measures the strength and direction of the *linear* relationship between two numerical variables $X$ and $Y$.

The formula is:

$$
r = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{(n-1) \cdot s_x \cdot s_y}
$$

where $s_x$ and $s_y$ are the sample standard deviations of $X$ and $Y$ respectively. In practice, software computes this for us — but understanding the formula gives insight into what $r$ actually measures: it asks how often $x$ and $y$ are *both* above their respective means (positive product) or both below (also positive), versus one above and one below (negative product).

The key properties of $r$:
- $-1 \leq r \leq 1$ always
- $r > 0$: positive linear association; $r < 0$: negative linear association
- $r = 1$ or $r = -1$: perfect linear relationship (all points on a line)
- $r = 0$: no *linear* relationship (but there could still be a curved relationship)
- $r$ is unitless and unaffected by changes in scale or location

### Spearman's Rank Correlation

Pearson's $r$ assumes a *linear* relationship and works best when both variables are roughly normally distributed without extreme outliers. When these conditions are not met — for example, when the data are ordinal, heavily skewed, or contain outliers that would distort a linear measure — we turn to **Spearman's rank correlation coefficient**, denoted $r_s$.

Spearman's $r_s$ is computed by first converting each variable's values to ranks (1 = smallest, $n$ = largest), then computing Pearson's $r$ on those ranks. A simplified formula is:

$$
r_s = 1 - \frac{6 \sum d_i^2}{n(n^2 - 1)}
$$

where $d_i$ is the difference between the ranks of the $i$-th observation in $X$ and $Y$.

Like Pearson's $r$, Spearman's $r_s$ ranges from $-1$ to $+1$. The difference is that Spearman's $r_s$ measures *monotonic* association — whether $Y$ tends to increase as $X$ increases, regardless of whether the relationship is linear.

### Key Points – Pearson and Spearman Correlation
- Pearson's $r$ measures the strength and direction of a *linear* relationship; ranges from $-1$ to $+1$
- $r = 0$ indicates no linear relationship, but a strong curved relationship may still exist
- Pearson's $r$ is sensitive to outliers and assumes roughly linear, normally distributed data
- Spearman's $r_s$ measures *monotonic* association using ranks — more robust to outliers and non-normality
- Both coefficients share the $[-1, 1]$ range and the same directional interpretation
- Neither coefficient alone establishes causation — only appropriate study design can do that
