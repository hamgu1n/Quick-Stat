## Section 10.2 – Interpreting Correlation Coefficients

Computing a correlation coefficient is straightforward; interpreting it correctly is where students often go wrong. In this section we address the most common pitfalls, the difference between association and causation, the effect of outliers, and what it means to test whether a correlation is statistically significant.

### Strength Guidelines

While there are no universal rules, the following guidelines are widely used to describe the strength of a correlation:

| $|r|$ value | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| 0.00 – 0.19 | Very weak or no association |
| 0.20 – 0.39 | Weak association |
| 0.40 – 0.59 | Moderate association |
| 0.60 – 0.79 | Strong association |
| 0.80 – 1.00 | Very strong association |

These are guidelines, not rules. The appropriate threshold for "strong" depends on the field, an $r$ of 0.5 might be impressive in psychology but unacceptably weak in physics.

### Correlation Does Not Imply Causation

This is perhaps the most important lesson in all of statistics: **a high correlation between two variables does not mean that one causes the other.** We saw a hint of this in Section 4.4 with the ice cream and drowning example. Here we formalize the idea.

Three possible explanations for an observed correlation between $X$ and $Y$ are:

1. $X$ causes $Y$
2. $Y$ causes $X$
3. A third variable $Z$ (a **confounding variable** or **lurking variable**) causes both $X$ and $Y$

The ice cream and drowning example is driven by a lurking variable: hot weather. Hot weather causes people to buy more ice cream *and* causes more people to swim, increasing drowning risk. Ice cream consumption doesn't cause drowning.

Only a properly designed experiment (with randomization and control, as discussed in Chapter 2) can establish causation. A correlation from an observational study, no matter how strong, cannot.

### The Effect of Outliers on Correlation

Pearson's $r$ is sensitive to outliers, as we noted in the previous section. A single unusual point can dramatically increase or decrease $r$, even when the rest of the data show no clear linear pattern. This is one reason why always *looking at the scatterplot* before reporting $r$ is critical. The correlation alone can be deeply misleading.

### Testing Whether a Correlation Is Significant

A computed correlation coefficient is a sample statistic, it varies from sample to sample. Just because $r \neq 0$ in our sample doesn't guarantee that the true population correlation $\rho$ (the Greek letter "rho") is nonzero. We can perform a hypothesis test:

$$
H_0: \rho = 0 \quad \text{(no linear relationship in the population)}
$$
$$
H_a: \rho \neq 0 \quad \text{(or one-sided)}
$$

The test statistic is:

$$
t = \frac{r\sqrt{n-2}}{\sqrt{1-r^2}}
$$

which follows a t-distribution with $n - 2$ degrees of freedom under $H_0$. The p-value tells us whether the observed $r$ is large enough, for the given sample size, to be statistically unlikely if there truly is no relationship.

### Key Points – Interpreting Correlation Coefficients
- Strength guidelines: $|r| < 0.20$ weak, $0.40$ – $0.59$ moderate, $\geq 0.80$ very strong (field-dependent)
- Correlation describes association, it does not establish causation
- A lurking variable may drive both variables simultaneously, producing correlation without direct causation
- Always inspect the scatterplot, outliers can dramatically distort $r$
- Statistical significance of $r$ depends on both the size of $r$ and the sample size $n$
- A significant correlation with a large sample may still represent a practically unimportant relationship, always consider effect size alongside p-values
