## Section 11.1 – The Least Squares Method

Correlation tells us the *strength* and *direction* of a linear relationship, but it doesn't give us a way to make predictions. If we know that hours studied and exam scores are strongly correlated, we still can't say "if a student studies 6 hours, what score should we expect?" For that, we need a line — specifically, the **least squares regression line**.

### The Simple Linear Regression Model

**Simple linear regression** models the relationship between one explanatory variable $X$ and one response variable $Y$ using a straight line:

$$
\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x
$$

where $\hat{y}$ (read "y-hat") is the **predicted value** of $Y$ for a given $X$, $b_0$ is the **y-intercept**, and $b_1$ is the **slope** — the predicted change in $Y$ for a one-unit increase in $X$.

The hat on $\hat{y}$ is important: it reminds us that we are predicting an *average* response, not an exact one. Individual observations will vary around the line.

### Finding the Least Squares Line

Among all possible straight lines we could draw through a scatterplot, the **least squares line** is the one that minimizes the sum of the squared **residuals**. A **residual** for observation $i$ is the difference between the observed and predicted values:

$$
e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i
$$

The least squares line minimizes $\sum e_i^2$ — the sum of squared residuals. The formulas for the slope and intercept are:

$$
b_1 = r \cdot \frac{s_y}{s_x}
$$

$$
b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1 \bar{x}
$$

where $r$ is Pearson's correlation, $s_y$ and $s_x$ are the sample standard deviations of $Y$ and $X$, and $\bar{y}$, $\bar{x}$ are the sample means. Note that the regression line always passes through the point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ — the means of the two variables.

### Extrapolation

The regression line is only reliable for predictions within the range of the observed data — or close to it. **Extrapolation** means making predictions far outside the range of $X$ values used to fit the model. This is dangerous because we have no evidence the linear trend continues beyond our data. For example, if our study hours data ranged from 1 to 10 hours, predicting the score for 50 hours of studying is extrapolation — the relationship might level off, reverse, or behave completely differently.

### Key Points – Least Squares Method
- The least squares line minimizes the sum of squared residuals: $\sum (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2$
- Slope: $b_1 = r \cdot s_y / s_x$ — the predicted change in $Y$ per one-unit increase in $X$
- Intercept: $b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1\bar{x}$ — the predicted $Y$ when $X = 0$
- The line always passes through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$
- Residuals measure prediction error: $e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i$; positive residuals lie above the line, negative below
- Avoid extrapolation — predictions far outside the range of observed $X$ values are unreliable
