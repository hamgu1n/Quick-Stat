## Section 11.2 – The Coefficient of Determination

The least squares regression line gives us the best linear prediction of $Y$ from $X$. But how good is that prediction, really? The **coefficient of determination**, denoted $R^2$ (or $r^2$ in simple linear regression), measures exactly this, the proportion of the total variability in $Y$ that is explained by the linear relationship with $X$.

### What Is $R^2$?

The idea behind $R^2$ is to compare two scenarios:

1. **No model**: our best prediction of $Y$ is simply its mean $\bar{y}$. The total variability in $Y$ around this baseline is called the **total sum of squares (SST)**:

$$
\text{SST} = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \bar{y})^2
$$

2. **With the regression model**: our prediction is $\hat{y}_i$. The remaining variability, the variability *not* explained by the model, is the **residual sum of squares (SSE)**:

$$
\text{SSE} = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2
$$

The coefficient of determination is:

$$
R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{SSE}}{\text{SST}} = \frac{\text{SST} - \text{SSE}}{\text{SST}}
$$

In simple linear regression, $R^2 = r^2$, the square of the Pearson correlation coefficient. So if $r = 0.9$, then $R^2 = 0.81$, meaning 81% of the variability in $Y$ is explained by its linear relationship with $X$.

$R^2$ ranges from 0 to 1. An $R^2$ of 0 means the model explains none of the variability (the regression line is no better than just using $\bar{y}$). An $R^2$ of 1 means the model explains all the variability (every point falls exactly on the line).

### $R^2$ Does Not Tell the Whole Story

A high $R^2$ sounds reassuring, but it does not guarantee the regression model is appropriate. Several cautions apply:

**A high $R^2$ does not prove causation.** As we emphasized in Chapter 10, a strong linear relationship may be driven by lurking variables.

**A high $R^2$ does not mean the model fits well.** If the true relationship is curved, fitting a straight line may still produce a high $R^2$ while systematically mispredicting across the range of $X$. This is why we examine residual plots (Section 11.3).

**A low $R^2$ does not make a model useless.** In complex fields like psychology or economics, $R^2$ values of 0.10 to 0.30 are common and the model may still be meaningful, especially if the slope is statistically significant and the relationship is theoretically justified.

### Key Points – Coefficient of Determination
- $R^2$ measures the proportion of variability in $Y$ explained by the linear relationship with $X$
- $R^2 = 1 - \text{SSE}/\text{SST}$; ranges from 0 to 1; in simple regression, $R^2 = r^2$
- $R^2 = 1$: perfect fit; $R^2 = 0$: model no better than using $\bar{y}$
- A high $R^2$ does not imply causation or guarantee the model is appropriate
- A low $R^2$ does not automatically mean the model is useless, significance and context matter
- Always examine residual plots alongside $R^2$ to assess whether a linear model is appropriate
