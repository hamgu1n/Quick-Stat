## Section 11.3 – Assumptions and Diagnostics

A regression line can be calculated for any two variables, but that doesn't mean the results will be meaningful. Like many statistical procedures, simple linear regression rests on a set of assumptions. When those assumptions are violated, the estimates of the slope and intercept may be unreliable, predictions may be poor, and p-values and confidence intervals may be misleading. In this section we identify the key assumptions and learn how to check them using **diagnostic plots**.

### The Assumptions of Simple Linear Regression

The four main assumptions are often summarized by the acronym **LINE**:

**L – Linearity**: The true relationship between $X$ and $Y$ is linear. If the real relationship is curved, a straight line will systematically over- or under-predict in certain regions.

**I – Independence**: The observations are independent of one another. This is typically satisfied by random sampling. Time series data or clustered data often violate this assumption.

**N – Normality**: The residuals (errors) are normally distributed. This allows us to construct valid confidence intervals and hypothesis tests for the slope. With large samples, this assumption matters less due to the CLT.

**E – Equal variance** (homoscedasticity): The spread of the residuals is constant across all values of $X$. When the spread increases or decreases systematically (called **heteroscedasticity**), standard errors and p-values may be unreliable.

### Checking Assumptions with Residual Plots

The most useful diagnostic tool is the **residual plot**, a scatterplot of the residuals ($e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i$) on the y-axis versus the fitted values ($\hat{y}_i$) on the x-axis.

A well-behaved residual plot shows:
- Points scattered randomly around the horizontal line at zero
- No visible pattern, curve, or trend
- Roughly constant vertical spread across all fitted values

If the residual plot shows a curve, the linearity assumption is likely violated. If the spread fans out as $\hat{y}$ increases (a "megaphone" shape), the equal-variance assumption is likely violated.

A **normal Q-Q plot** of the residuals checks the normality assumption. If the residuals are normally distributed, the points will fall approximately along a straight diagonal line.

### Influential Points and Leverage

We discussed outliers in the context of scatterplots back in Section 4.4. In regression, certain outliers have an outsized effect on the fitted line. An observation is said to have **high leverage** if its $x$-value is unusually far from $\bar{x}$, it sits at the edge of the data and has the potential to pull the regression line in its direction. A point is **influential** if removing it substantially changes the estimated slope.

Not every high-leverage point is influential (it might happen to lie close to the overall trend), and not every outlier is high-leverage (it might have an average $x$-value but an unusual $y$-value). Both types deserve attention before finalizing a model.

### Key Points – Assumptions and Diagnostics
- The LINE assumptions for regression: Linearity, Independence, Normality of residuals, Equal variance (homoscedasticity)
- Residual plots (residuals vs. fitted values) are the primary tool for checking linearity and equal variance
- A good residual plot shows random scatter around zero with constant spread
- The normal Q-Q plot checks the normality of residuals
- High-leverage points have extreme $x$-values; influential points substantially change the estimated slope when removed
- Always check assumptions before trusting regression output, a line can be fit to any data, but that doesn't make it appropriate
